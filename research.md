## Molecular Clock Mechanisms

The lab has made foundational contributions to the molecular logic of the mammalian clock,
including discovery and characterization of BMAL1/MOP3 and later work on regulators and
feedback structure in mammalian circadian transcription. These studies helped define the
positive limb of the mammalian oscillator and the broader regulatory architecture surrounding
clock-controlled gene expression.

## Functional Genomics and the Circadian Transcriptome

The lab has used genome-scale experimental approaches to define regulators and outputs of the
circadian system. This includes influential work on the mammalian circadian transcriptome and
functional genomics screens that identified pathways and genes involved in clock regulation.
These datasets helped establish that circadian transcription is extensive, tissue-specific,
and biologically integrated with broader physiological networks.

## Circadian Atlas and Public Resources

A major public contribution has been the circadian gene expression atlas across mouse organs,
which showed the extent and organ specificity of rhythmic transcription in mammals. The lab
has also built and disseminated community resources for rhythmic biology, including JTK_CYCLE,
PSEA, MetaCycle, CYCLOPS, and CircaDB, so that other investigators can analyze periodic data,
interpret phase-structured pathways, and explore public circadian datasets.

## Human Circadian Transcriptomics

Published work from the lab has extended circadian transcriptomics into human studies,
including analyses that use CYCLOPS to recover temporal structure from unordered samples.
These public studies helped demonstrate the feasibility and value of human circadian
transcriptomics for understanding physiology, disease, and the broader rationale for
circadian medicine and chronotherapy.
